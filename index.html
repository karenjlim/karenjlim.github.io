Case Study 01 · SHS Point-of-Care Diagnostics

Device Harmonization Across
Primary and Critical Care

Siemens Healthineers · Atellica® Portfolio · 2015–2023 · 8 years

Siemens Healthineers POC diagnostic devices had grown through independent product teams, each with its own workflow, interaction model, and visual language. Clinicians moving between devices had to relearn every time. I worked to get alignment on a unified approach — built from scratch, with misaligned incentives at every level.

RoleLed UX research and interaction design across an 8-year, multi-device portfolio. Directed external vendors and an internal team of 2–3 across engineering, product, and regulatory. Named patent inventor.
Scope10+ devices developed simultaneously by separate teams. 13 languages, 3 primary screen sizes with additional legacy and acquisition variants. Devices deployed in ORs, ICUs, and EDs across Critical Care and Chronic Disease Management lines.
ImpactField research identified patient/sample mismatch as a design gap, not user error. The finding became a feature. The feature became a patent.

Challenges

  • 01Multiple Device Types. Each device had evolved independently, with different workflows, interface conventions, and interaction models across Critical Care and Chronic Disease Management lines.
  • 02Training Complexity. CLIA regulations tie personnel qualifications to test complexity. Any harmonization had to preserve these distinctions in the UI, not collapse them.
  • 03Environment as Risk. A 10-second interruption in a physician office is trivial. In an ICU during a procedure, it's a patient risk. Designing for the device without designing for the environment was the root failure of the existing interfaces.
  • 04Regulatory Constraints. Every design decision required validation against FDA requirements. Changes had to be provably safer, not just simpler.

Research

  • →Contextual Inquiry. Direct observation at 12+ clinical sites. Lab studies missed it — field studies found it. Clinical staff under pressure behave completely differently than in a controlled usability setting: workarounds, interruptions, and error recovery patterns that never surface in a lab.
  • →Usability Testing. 15+ formal studies. Time on task, error rates, success rates. Caught critical issues before costly development cycles.
  • →Visual Validation. Ensured hierarchy and clarity met accuracy standards across all device types. Can users read and trust this interface under pressure?
  • →Stakeholder Triangulation. Cross-referenced perspectives across clinical, engineering, and regulatory stakeholders to surface tensions invisible to any single group.

Clinical office and perfusionist station in a cardiac OR — research conducted where the work actually happens. Paper prototype session for workflow validation. Stakeholder workshop surfacing clinical, engineering, and regulatory perspectives in the same room.

Findings

The hardest finding to land — most findings get documented. This one changed the product.

Mismatch is a design problem, not the user's

Patient/sample mismatch — the #1 error type — was not a lapse in attention. The interface provided no structural safeguard at the exact moment where mismatch was most likely. The error was designed in.

Complexity is a patient safety risk

Interface complexity in a clinical setting doesn't just frustrate — it creates conditions for critical errors. Reducing interaction steps and visual noise wasn't a UX preference. It was a safety requirement.

Harmonization ≠ homogenization

Shared interaction patterns are achievable and valuable, but workflow specificity is non-negotiable. Forcing a single flow onto devices with fundamentally different clinical contexts would have broken use.

Trust comes from clarity, not features

Users wanted precise, consistent feedback at each step. Simple confirmations — done right — built the clinical confidence they needed to trust the device under pressure.

Interaction Design

Interaction design for the full test workflow — from operator login through sample acquisition, patient ID entry, test execution, and result review.

Each step mapped to the exact clinical sequence observed in field research. The workflow comparison matrix identified which interaction steps could be unified across devices and which required device-specific handling due to clinical context differences.

Operator ID and device state visibility were redesigned to reduce cognitive load at the points where patient/sample mismatch was most likely.

Visual System

Color palette, typography, and icon system developed to meet Siemens Healthineers brand standards while satisfying clinical accuracy requirements — sufficient contrast, unambiguous status indicators, and a consistent icon language across the full device portfolio.

All UI components specified in a formal guidelines document used across product teams for FDA regulatory alignment.

Case Study 02 · HELX Touch Ultrasound

Complete UI Redesign of a Premium Ultrasound Platform

Siemens Corporate Research · Healthcare Ultrasound · 2013–2015

HELX Touch was one of the larger engagements — a full UI redesign of a premium ultrasound platform that had lost ground to GE and Philips.

RoleBrought on as UX Research & Design Lead; took over as Project Manager mid-project when the PM left, continuing research and design work while running the project.
Scope6 iterative cycles over 2 years across 4 countries. 36 contextual interviews across the US, Germany, France, and China. 6 usability studies with 16–24 participants each. 390 web survey participants across 5 countries. User research, market research, workflow analysis, hardware design, interaction design, visual design, and usability testing.
Impact75% sonographer preference over GE and Philips in an independent study. 4× preorders vs. previous release. #1 comparative preference across all countries studied.

Challenges

  • 01Lost competitive ground. The platform had fallen behind GE and Philips. The redesign had to close that gap — not just incrementally, but decisively enough to show up in preference studies and preorders.
  • 02No project manager for the second half of the engagement. Coordinating across disciplines and managing client expectations while continuing research and design work.
  • 03Hardware and software had to move together. The control panel redesign wasn't just a UI problem — physical layout had to align with the on-screen structure, which meant coordinating across disciplines.
  • 04Unfamiliar domain, short timeline. A consultancy environment meant new domain, a client who needed convincing, and no room for a slow start.

Research

  • →Field Observations. On-site observation of sonographers in clinical settings to understand real workflow, not assumed workflow. Sonographers use far fewer features than the interface offered — find the target, measure, annotate, done. The redesign prioritized the actual workflow, not the full feature set.
  • →Conference & Academic Research. Industry trends and emerging interaction patterns informing design direction.
  • →Market Analysis. Competitive benchmarking against GE and Philips to identify gaps and opportunities.
  • →Iterative Usability Testing. Each cycle produced quantitative feedback — preference ratings, task metrics — and qualitative findings that drove the next iteration. 12 control panel concepts tested across cycles. A fan-shaped layout consistently outperformed others on reach and comfort — that became the foundation. Cross-cultural facilitation required adjustment: German participants gave their most candid feedback after sessions ended; Chinese participants required reading between the lines; American participants were direct throughout.
  • →Web Survey. Validating preference and direction at scale across all countries studied.

Site visit in China showing how tightly clustered a sonographer's workspace can be. Foam board control panel concept used in hands-on testing. Prototype evaluations at two different stages of iteration — same participants, evolving fidelity.

Outcome

Imaging UI — 4 clear sections

Restructured the imaging workspace into 4 distinct zones with clear visual hierarchy. Reduced mode-switching friction for sonographers.

Annotations UI — body markers

Touch-optimized body marker selection, replacing cumbersome hardware controls with an intuitive on-screen workflow.

Control panel — simplified layout

Less-used controls moved to the touch screen. Improved control placement, grouping, and spacing. Simplified home base with fewer soft keys. Physical layout aligned with the on-screen UI structure.

Case Study 03 · Building Technology — Product Family Harmonization

Harmonizing a Building Control Product Family Across 5 Screen Types

Siemens Corporate Research · Building Technologies & Infrastructure · 2011–2013

A cross-product harmonization brief for Siemens CPS products, early in my time at Corporate Research. The goal: create a unified approach to the interaction between users and Siemens CPS products, define a common look and feel, and support more efficient product development across the family.

RoleUX/UI Designer across the full product family. User research, market research, workflow analysis, hardware design, interaction design, and visual design.
Scope2 years · 5 screen types across a single product family, from consumer thermostats to enterprise data center management. Consumer to administrator user types.
ImpactAll 5 product types implemented. Validated with facility managers and IT administrators. The framework thinking developed here directly shaped how I approached the SHS POC DX platform years later.

Challenges

  • 01Different screen technologies. Five screen types with fundamentally different hardware constraints, resolutions, and interaction models — each requiring a different implementation of the same design logic.
  • 02Different user types. Consumer to enterprise administrator — the same product family had to work for someone adjusting a room thermostat and someone managing a data center.
  • 03Different use contexts. Thermostats, data center management, mobile apps — radically different environments and task structures that still needed to share a coherent interaction framework.

Research

  • →Workflow analysis. Mapped task sequences and usage patterns across all screen types to identify where a shared structure was feasible and where device-specific handling was required.
  • →User testing across user types. Validated the shared layout system with both consumer users and enterprise administrators — confirming that the same structural logic held across radically different use contexts.
  • →Iterative design and testing. The shared layout — status bar, navigation, work area — was tested across screen sizes and orientations. Navigation placement (landscape left, portrait bottom) was validated to remain consistent and learnable across devices.

Outcome

Implemented across all 5 product types

Validated with user groups ranging from facility managers to IT administrators.

Framework over template

A shared structure — status bar, navigation, work area — implemented consistently across all five screen types, from consumer thermostats to enterprise data center management.

The existing product family — each with its own interface logic. Competitive positioning across functionality and user type. The unified framework applied across building control and data center management. Final outputs sharing a coherent visual language for the first time.

Case Study 04 · Oil & Gas — Pressure Protection Management

Redesigning Industrial Software That Took Months to Learn

Siemens Corporate Research · Energy / Oil & Gas · 2014

PPM — software used by engineers to specify oil refineries and run safety analysis — was built internally with no UX direction. Users needed months of training. Complaints were growing.

RoleUX Project Manager. Led user research, workflow analysis, and 3 iterative design cycles with testing. New domain — learned the engineering workflows from scratch.
ScopeEngineers across oil refinery and safety analysis workflows · Domain required learning pressure protection from scratch.
ImpactAn interface built around how engineers actually work, not how the system was built. The new interaction framework became the design standard for subsequent internal tools.

Challenges

  • 01Interface organized around system architecture, not user tasks. Engineers had to navigate by system logic rather than their actual work sequence.
  • 02Invisible data transfer between pages. Workflow depended on data moving between pages that was invisible and undocumented in the UI — engineers had no way to know what was happening.
  • 03No hierarchy, no prioritization, no wayfinding. Everything lived on a single level inside accordion menus. The most important features were the hardest to reach, and the least important were always visible.

Research

  • →User research. Interviewed engineers to understand pressure protection workflows and where the current interface broke down.
  • →Workflow analysis. Mapped the actual task sequence engineers followed to identify the gap between how the interface was organized and how work actually happened. The entire navigation structure needed to be rebuilt around three phases that matched how engineers actually worked: building the model, analyzing it, and reporting results.
  • →Iterative design and testing. 3 design cycles, each validated with engineers from the target user group. In the first cycle, 3 concepts were tested head-to-head. The heat maps to the right show task completion patterns across 8 participants, identifying where each concept broke down before the next iteration began.

Outcome

Stable, simple navigation

Followed user workflow rather than system architecture. The redesign produced a complete information architecture covering every section of the tool — the first time the full scope of the interface had been mapped and organized as a whole.

Multiple panes for functional grouping

Key features always visible but not prominent. Simplified input fields throughout.

New interaction framework

Task-based navigation, visible data state, and prioritized feature access — applied consistently enough that subsequent teams adopted it as the starting point for new tools.
